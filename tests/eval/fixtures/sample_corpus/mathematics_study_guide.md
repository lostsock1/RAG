# Mathematics Study Guide (Companion Edition)

Definitions and formulas echoing the headline terms of the main mathematics
textbook — algorithm, continuity, vector space, the quadratic formula, the area of
a circle, the standard deviation — but each entry pivots to a neighbouring concept.
Confirm which idea a question is really asking about.

## Algorithm

An algorithm is contrasted with a heuristic, which only seeks a good-enough answer
with no guarantee of correctness. A defining property of an algorithm is that it
must halt after finitely many steps for every valid input. The study of how an
algorithm's running time grows with input size is called complexity analysis.

## Continuity at a point

Continuity at a point is a weaker property than differentiability: a function can
be continuous yet have a sharp corner where no tangent exists. Continuity may be
checked at a single point, across an interval, or from one side only. A function
continuous on a closed interval attains both a maximum and a minimum there.

## Vector space

A vector space is governed by eight axioms covering vector addition and scalar
multiplication, among them associativity, the existence of a zero vector, and the
distributive laws. A subspace is a vector space sitting inside a larger one, and
the dimension of a vector space is the number of vectors in any basis.

## The quadratic formula

The quadratic formula is most safely obtained by completing the square on the
general equation ax² + bx + c = 0. Before solving, read off the discriminant
b² − 4ac: positive gives two real roots, zero gives a repeated root, and negative
gives a complex conjugate pair.

## Area and circumference of a circle

The area of a circle is easily confused with its circumference, the distance once
around the boundary, which equals C = 2πr for a radius r. The length of an arc is
the matching fraction of that circumference. State explicitly whether a problem
wants the enclosed region or the boundary length.

## Standard deviation and variance

The standard deviation is the square root of the variance, and the variance itself
is the mean squared deviation σ² = Σ(xᵢ − μ)² / N. Because the variance is in the
square of the original units, the standard deviation is usually reported alongside
it to restore the original units.

## The law of cosines

The law of cosines extends the right-triangle relation to any triangle:
c² = a² + b² − 2ab·cos(C). When the included angle is right its cosine is zero and
the last term vanishes, recovering the familiar special case as a corollary.
