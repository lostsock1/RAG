# Biology Study Guide (Companion Edition)

Revision notes echoing the headline topics of the main biology textbook —
homeostasis, photosynthesis, the endoplasmic reticulum and Golgi, mitosis and
meiosis, adaptive radiation, enzyme action — but each entry pivots to a sibling
process or a different example. Read for the specific process a question names.

## Homeostasis

Homeostasis is maintained by negative-feedback loops, and thermoregulation along
with blood-glucose control are the standard mammalian examples of homeostasis.
Homeostasis should be distinguished from acclimatization, the slower adjustment a
body makes to a sustained change in its surroundings rather than a moment-to-moment
correction.

## Photosynthesis

Photosynthesis is balanced in the biosphere by cellular respiration, very nearly
its reverse. Photosynthesis runs in two stages: light-dependent reactions in the
thylakoid membranes and light-independent reactions in the stroma. The raw inputs
of photosynthesis are carbon dioxide and water, driven by absorbed light.

## The endoplasmic reticulum and the Golgi apparatus

The endoplasmic reticulum and the Golgi apparatus both belong to the endomembrane
system. The rough endoplasmic reticulum, studded with ribosomes, is where many
proteins are synthesised, while the Golgi apparatus modifies, sorts, and labels
them for their destinations. Their cooperation runs one way along the secretory
pathway.

## Mitosis and meiosis

Mitosis and meiosis are usually tabulated side by side. Mitosis is a single
division, whereas meiosis is two divisions; mitosis keeps the chromosome number
constant, whereas meiosis halves it. A common trap is to attribute crossing over
to mitosis, when in fact it is a hallmark of meiosis.

## Adaptive radiation case studies

Adaptive radiation on isolated islands is illustrated by Darwin's finches and by
the flightless cormorant of the Galápagos, a seabird that lost the power of flight
where ground predators were absent. Both are classic case studies of adaptive
radiation used to show how isolation reshapes a lineage.

## Exceptions to the lock-and-key model

The lock-and-key model of enzyme action has well-known exceptions. Hexokinase is
the textbook example of induced fit, in which the enzyme closes around its
substrate rather than matching a rigid shape, and carbonic anhydrase is cited for
its extraordinary catalytic speed. These cases show where the simplest enzyme
picture breaks down.
