# Chapter 4: Chemical Reactions and Bonding

## 4.1 Chemical Reactions: An Overview

A chemical reaction is a process in which one or more substances (reactants) are transformed into one or more different substances (products). Chemical reactions involve the breaking and forming of chemical bonds, resulting in changes to the arrangement of atoms. Reactions can be classified into several broad categories: synthesis (combination), decomposition, single displacement, double displacement, and combustion.

The rate at which a chemical reaction proceeds depends on several factors, including temperature, concentration of reactants, surface area, and the presence or absence of a catalyst. Understanding and controlling reaction rates is central to industrial chemistry, biochemistry, and materials science.

## 4.2 Catalysts

A **catalyst** is a substance that increases the **reaction rate** of a chemical reaction without itself being consumed in the process. Catalysts work by providing an alternative reaction pathway with a lower **activation energy** — the minimum energy barrier that must be overcome for reactants to be converted into products.

### How Catalysts Work

In any chemical reaction, reactant molecules must collide with sufficient energy and proper orientation for the reaction to occur. The activation energy (E_a) represents the energy hill that must be climbed. A catalyst lowers this hill by forming an intermediate complex with the reactants, stabilizing the transition state, and thereby reducing the energy required to reach it.

Importantly, a catalyst is **not consumed** during the reaction. Although it may participate in intermediate steps, it is regenerated in its original form by the end of the catalytic cycle. This means that a small amount of catalyst can facilitate the transformation of large quantities of reactants, making catalysis economically and practically valuable.

### Types of Catalysts

1. **Homogeneous catalysts:** These exist in the same phase as the reactants. For example, acid catalysts in aqueous solution catalyze ester hydrolysis.

2. **Heterogeneous catalysts:** These exist in a different phase from the reactants, typically a solid catalyst with liquid or gaseous reactants. The catalytic converter in automobiles uses platinum and palladium to convert toxic exhaust gases into less harmful products.

3. **Enzymes:** Biological catalysts (proteins or RNA) that are remarkably specific and efficient. Enzymes catalyze virtually all biochemical reactions in living organisms. They operate under mild conditions (physiological temperature and pH) and can accelerate reactions by factors of 10⁶ to 10¹².

### Catalysis and Equilibrium

A fundamental point about catalysts is that they accelerate both the forward and reverse reactions equally. They do not change the position of chemical equilibrium or the amount of product formed at equilibrium — they simply allow equilibrium to be reached more quickly. The thermodynamic favorability of a reaction (determined by the Gibbs free energy change, ΔG) is unaffected by the presence of a catalyst.

### Industrial Importance

Catalysis is essential to modern industry. The Haber-Bosch process uses an iron catalyst to produce ammonia from nitrogen and hydrogen, feeding billions of people through fertilizer production. The contact process uses vanadium pentoxide to catalyze sulfuric acid production. Petroleum refining relies on zeolite catalysts for cracking heavy hydrocarbons into lighter, more useful products.

## 4.3 Chemical Bonding

Chemical bonds are the forces that hold atoms together in molecules and compounds. The three primary types of chemical bonds are ionic bonds, covalent bonds, and metallic bonds. The type of bond formed between atoms depends on the electronegativity difference between them and the nature of the orbitals involved.

### Ionic Bonding

Ionic bonds form when one atom transfers one or more electrons to another atom, creating oppositely charged ions that are held together by electrostatic attraction. This type of bonding typically occurs between metals (which lose electrons to form cations) and nonmetals (which gain electrons to form anions). Sodium chloride (NaCl) is the classic example: sodium donates an electron to chlorine, forming Na⁺ and Cl⁻ ions.

### Covalent Bonding

A **covalent bond** is a chemical bond formed when two **atoms** **share** one or more pairs of **electrons**. Unlike ionic bonding, where electrons are transferred, covalent bonding involves the mutual sharing of electrons between atoms to achieve a stable electron configuration (typically an octet).

In a covalent bond, the shared electron pair occupies the region between the two nuclei, and the electrostatic attraction between the negatively charged electrons and the positively charged nuclei holds the atoms together. The simplest example is the hydrogen molecule (H₂), in which two hydrogen atoms share a pair of electrons.

#### Types of Covalent Bonds

- **Single bond:** One shared electron pair (e.g., H-H, Cl-Cl)
- **Double bond:** Two shared electron pairs (e.g., O=O in molecular oxygen)
- **Triple bond:** Three shared electron pairs (e.g., N≡N in molecular nitrogen)

#### Polar vs. Nonpolar Covalent Bonds

When the two atoms sharing electrons have equal or nearly equal electronegativity, the electron pair is shared equally, forming a **nonpolar covalent bond**. When there is a significant electronegativity difference, the shared electrons are drawn closer to the more electronegative atom, creating a **polar covalent bond** with partial charges (δ+ and δ−) on the atoms.

For example, in HCl, chlorine is more electronegative than hydrogen, so the shared electron pair is shifted toward chlorine, making the bond polar.

### Metallic Bonding

In metallic bonding, valence electrons are delocalized across a lattice of metal cations, forming an "electron sea." This model explains the characteristic properties of metals: electrical conductivity, malleability, ductility, and thermal conductivity.

## 4.4 Bond Energy and Reaction Enthalpy

The strength of a chemical bond is measured by its bond dissociation energy — the energy required to break the bond homolytically. Bond energies are typically expressed in kilojoules per mole (kJ/mol).

| Bond Type | Bond Energy (kJ/mol) |
|-----------|---------------------|
| C-C       | 347                 |
| C=C       | 614                 |
| C≡C       | 839                 |
| C-H       | 413                 |
| O-H       | 463                 |
| N≡N       | 945                 |
| H-H       | 436                 |

The enthalpy change of a reaction (ΔH) can be estimated by comparing the total bond energies of the reactants with those of the products. If the products have stronger bonds overall than the reactants, the reaction is exothermic (ΔH < 0). Conversely, if the products have weaker bonds, the reaction is endothermic (ΔH > 0).

## 4.5 Reaction Mechanisms

A reaction mechanism describes the step-by-step sequence of elementary reactions by which a chemical transformation occurs. Most chemical reactions do not proceed in a single step but through a series of intermediate stages. Each elementary step has its own activation energy barrier.

The slowest step in a mechanism is called the **rate-determining step** because it limits the overall reaction rate. Catalysts often work by providing an alternative mechanism with a lower-energy rate-determining step.

## Chapter Summary

This chapter explored chemical reactions and the bonding that underlies them. We examined catalysts — substances that increase reaction rate by lowering activation energy without being consumed — and the three major types of chemical bonds: ionic, covalent (where atoms share electrons), and metallic. Understanding these concepts is essential for predicting and controlling chemical behavior in both laboratory and industrial settings.
