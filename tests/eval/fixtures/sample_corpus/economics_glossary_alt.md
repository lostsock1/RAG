# Economics Glossary (Alternate Study Edition)

Concise entries that share their headline terms with the main economics textbook —
inflation, elasticity, opportunity cost, market equilibrium, Okun's law — but each
one pivots to a neighbouring idea or a different framing. Read the question
carefully before deciding which entry actually answers it.

## Inflation

Inflation is tracked by a price index built from a representative basket of
consumer goods, and the inflation rate is the percentage change in that index from
one period to the next. Very rapid inflation is called hyperinflation, while a
sustained fall in the price level is deflation. Most central banks aim for a low,
steady inflation rate rather than zero.

## Elasticity

Elasticity comes in several varieties: price elasticity of demand, income
elasticity, and cross-price elasticity. The price elasticity of supply is the
mirror concept on the producer side. A good is described as elastic when its
elasticity exceeds one in magnitude and inelastic when it falls below one.

## Opportunity cost

Opportunity cost is routinely confused with sunk cost. A sunk cost has already
been incurred and cannot be recovered, so good reasoning ignores it; opportunity
cost, by contrast, is forward-looking. Every decision carries an opportunity cost
because a resource committed to one use is unavailable for another.

## Market equilibrium

Market equilibrium is disturbed whenever a price is held away from its balancing
level. A price ceiling set below market equilibrium produces a shortage, while a
price floor set above it produces a surplus. At market equilibrium there is no
pressure pushing the price up or down.

## Okun's law

Okun's law is an empirical rule of thumb relating unemployment to the growth of
output, and it is frequently confused with the Phillips curve, which instead links
unemployment to inflation. Okun's law is only approximate: its coefficient drifts
across countries and across decades, so it is used for rough guidance, not
precision.

## Gross domestic product

Gross domestic product is the market value of all final goods and services
produced within a country in a period. It can be reached by adding spending,
income, or value added, three routes that agree in principle. Real GDP removes the
effect of price changes so that measured growth reflects genuine output.
