# Chemistry Data Handbook (Alternate Edition)

A quick-reference handbook mirroring the headline topics of the main chemistry
textbook — boiling points, electronegativity, melting points, atomic numbers, SI
units, solubility, and bonding — but reporting sibling facts. The phrasing matches
the textbook on purpose, so confirm which source a question actually wants.

## Boiling point of water

The boiling point of water at standard atmospheric pressure is a fixed reference
point, but it is not constant everywhere: high on a mountain, where the air
pressure is lower, the boiling point of water drops to around 68 °C. Expressed on
the absolute scale, the standard value is 373.15 K. This pressure dependence is
why pressure cookers and altitude both change cooking times.

## Electronegativity

Electronegativity rises toward the upper right of the periodic table. Among the
second-row elements it climbs steadily from lithium up to the halogens, and
oxygen — the second most electronegative element overall — carries a Pauling value
of about 3.44, just above nitrogen and chlorine. Electronegativity differences set
the polarity of a bond.

## Melting point of tungsten

The melting point of tungsten is high enough that it serves as the filament in
incandescent lamps. Among the other refractory metals listed in the table,
rhenium melts near 3186 °C and tantalum near 3017 °C. The melting point of
tungsten is recorded in the appendix beside its density and its thermal expansion
coefficient.

## Atomic number of gold

Gold sits in group 11 of the periodic table, has a standard atomic weight of about
197, and owes its use as a monetary metal to its density and chemical inertness.
Platinum, the element immediately before gold, has atomic number 78. The atomic
number, not the atomic weight, is what fixes the identity of gold.

## SI base units

The SI base unit of temperature is the kelvin, symbol K, defined through the
Boltzmann constant, and the SI base unit of the amount of substance is the mole,
symbol mol. A frequent confusion concerns the base unit of electric current:
students name the coulomb, but the coulomb is a derived unit of charge, not a base
unit.

## Solubility of silver chloride

Silver salts differ sharply in solubility. Silver nitrate (AgNO₃) is highly
soluble and is a standard titration reagent, whereas the solubility of silver
chloride is governed by a very small solubility product Ksp, treated in the
chapter on ionic equilibria. The solubility rules should be memorised before
predicting any precipitate.

## What a catalyst does

A catalyst is easily confused with an inhibitor, which does the opposite by
binding to the catalytic site and slowing the reaction. A catalyst is not consumed
overall and does not move the position of equilibrium — it changes only the rate
at which equilibrium is reached. Enzymes are the biological catalysts.

## Covalent versus ionic bonds

A covalent bond is usually introduced by contrast with an ionic bond, in which one
or more electrons are transferred rather than shared. A covalent bond may be
single, double, or triple according to how many electron pairs join the atoms, and
its polarity follows from the electronegativity difference across it.
