# Law Glossary (Alternate Study Edition)

Short entries sharing their headline terms with the main law textbook —
sovereignty, constitutional monarchy, civil liability, precedent — but each pivots
to a neighbouring distinction. The overlapping vocabulary is deliberate, so match
the question to the precise concept rather than the nearest word.

## Sovereignty

Sovereignty in international law has two faces: internal sovereignty, the supreme
authority a state holds within its own borders, and external sovereignty,
independence from outside control. In practice sovereignty is increasingly
qualified by treaty commitments and by membership in international organizations,
so it is rarely absolute.

## Constitutional monarchy

A constitutional monarchy is best understood by contrast. In an absolute monarchy
no constitution limits the crown; in a republic there is no monarch at all. A
constitutional monarchy occupies the middle ground, and the monarch's functions
are largely ceremonial while elected ministers govern.

## Civil liability

Civil liability is distinguished from criminal liability: civil liability is owed
to another private party, whereas criminal liability answers to the state and may
bring punishment. Civil liability can arise from a breach of contract or from a
tort, and it is the form of liability that private insurance commonly covers.

## Precedent

Precedent is the backbone of the common law, captured in the maxim stare decisis.
Within a judgment, only the ratio decidendi — the reasoning essential to the
result — is binding precedent, while remarks made in passing, the obiter dicta,
carry persuasive weight only. A sufficiently senior court may overrule an earlier
precedent.

## Statute and codification

A statute is a written law enacted by a legislature, in contrast to rules that
accumulate from the decisions of courts. Codification gathers scattered rules into
a single ordered instrument. Where a clear statute conflicts with an older
judge-made rule, the statute generally prevails.
