# Chapter 3: Thermodynamics, Fundamental Forces, and Electromagnetism

## 3.1 The Second Law of Thermodynamics

The second law of thermodynamics is one of the most fundamental principles in all of physics. It states that the total entropy of an **isolated system** never decreases over time. In any spontaneous process occurring within an isolated system, the entropy either increases or, in the idealized case of a reversible process, remains constant. This law establishes a preferred direction for natural processes — often called the **arrow of time** — and distinguishes between reversible and irreversible processes.

Entropy, denoted *S*, is a state function that quantifies the number of microscopic configurations (microstates) that correspond to a thermodynamic system's macroscopic state. Ludwig Boltzmann formalized this relationship as:

> *S = k_B ln Ω*

where *k_B* is the Boltzmann constant and Ω is the number of accessible microstates. The second law implies that natural processes tend to evolve toward states with higher thermodynamic probability — that is, states with more microstates.

### Practical Consequences

1. **Heat flow:** Heat spontaneously flows from a hotter body to a colder body, never the reverse, unless external work is performed.
2. **Engine efficiency:** No heat engine operating between two thermal reservoirs can be more efficient than a Carnot engine. The Carnot efficiency is η = 1 − T_cold / T_hot.
3. **Mixing:** When two gases are allowed to mix, the entropy of the combined system increases.
4. **Biological systems:** Living organisms maintain low internal entropy by exporting entropy to their surroundings, consistent with the second law applied to the organism-plus-environment system.

The second law of thermodynamics has profound implications for cosmology (heat death of the universe), information theory (Landauer's principle), and engineering (limits on energy conversion efficiency).

## 3.2 The Ideal Gas Law

The ideal gas law is a fundamental equation of state that relates the macroscopic properties of an ideal gas. It is expressed as:

> **PV = nRT**

where:

- **P** is the **pressure** of the gas (measured in pascals, Pa, or atmospheres, atm)
- **V** is the **volume** occupied by the gas (measured in cubic meters, m³, or liters, L)
- **n** is the number of moles of the gas
- **R** is the **gas constant** (8.314 J/(mol·K))
- **T** is the absolute temperature (measured in kelvin, K)

The ideal gas law combines several earlier empirical gas laws: Boyle's law (PV = constant at fixed T and n), Charles's law (V/T = constant at fixed P and n), and Avogadro's law (V/n = constant at fixed P and T). It assumes that gas molecules occupy negligible volume and exert no intermolecular forces — assumptions that are reasonable at low pressures and high temperatures.

### Deviations from Ideal Behavior

Real gases deviate from ideal behavior at high pressures and low temperatures. The van der Waals equation corrects for these deviations:

> (P + a n²/V²)(V − nb) = nRT

where *a* accounts for intermolecular attractions and *b* accounts for the finite volume of gas molecules.

## 3.3 Ohm's Law

Ohm's law describes the linear relationship between **voltage**, **current**, and **resistance** in an electrical circuit. It is stated as:

> **V = IR**

where:

- **V** is the **voltage** (electric potential difference) across the conductor, measured in volts (V)
- **I** is the **current** flowing through the conductor, measured in amperes (A)
- **R** is the **resistance** of the conductor, measured in ohms (Ω)

Ohm's law holds for ohmic materials — those whose resistance is independent of the applied voltage and current. Many common conductors (copper wire, resistors) are approximately ohmic over a wide range of conditions. Non-ohmic materials, such as semiconductors and the filament of an incandescent bulb, do not obey Ohm's law because their resistance changes with temperature or applied voltage.

### Applications

- **Circuit analysis:** Ohm's law is the foundation of Kirchhoff's voltage and current laws, which are used to analyze complex circuits.
- **Power dissipation:** The power dissipated by a resistor is P = IV = I²R = V²/R.
- **Sensor design:** Many sensors (thermistors, photoresistors) exploit the relationship between resistance and physical quantities.

## 3.4 The Gravitational Constant

Newton's law of universal gravitation states that every particle of matter attracts every other particle with a force proportional to the product of their masses and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between them:

> F = G × m₁ × m₂ / r²

The **gravitational constant G** is the proportionality constant in this equation. Its accepted value is:

> **G = 6.674 × 10⁻¹¹ N m²/kg²**

This constant was first measured with reasonable accuracy by Henry Cavendish in 1798 using a torsion balance experiment. The extremely small value of G explains why gravitational forces between everyday objects are negligible compared to electromagnetic forces. It is only when at least one of the masses is enormous (such as a planet or star) that gravity becomes the dominant force.

G is one of the fundamental physical constants and is known with less precision than most other fundamental constants, owing to the extreme difficulty of measuring gravitational forces in the laboratory. As of 2018, the CODATA recommended value is 6.67430(15) × 10⁻¹¹ N m²/kg², with a relative standard uncertainty of 2.2 × 10⁻⁵.

## 3.5 The Four Fundamental Forces of Nature

All known interactions in the universe can be described by four fundamental forces:

1. **Gravitational force:** The weakest of the four forces, but it acts over infinite range and is always attractive. It governs the large-scale structure of the universe — the orbits of planets, the formation of galaxies, and the dynamics of the cosmos. Described by Einstein's general theory of relativity.

2. **Electromagnetic force:** The force between charged particles. It acts over infinite range but can be both attractive and repulsive. Electromagnetic interactions are responsible for chemistry, the behavior of light, and virtually all everyday phenomena except gravity. Described by quantum electrodynamics (QED).

3. **Strong nuclear force:** The strongest of the four forces, but it acts only over very short ranges (~1 fm). It binds quarks together to form protons and neutrons, and it holds protons and neutrons together in atomic nuclei. Without the strong nuclear force, nuclei would disintegrate due to electromagnetic repulsion between protons. Described by quantum chromodynamics (QCD).

4. **Weak nuclear force:** Responsible for radioactive beta decay and other subatomic processes involving neutrinos. It acts over extremely short ranges (~0.001 fm) and is the only force that can change one type of quark into another. The weak nuclear force plays a crucial role in nuclear fusion reactions that power stars.

Physicists have unified the electromagnetic and weak nuclear forces into the electroweak theory. Grand unified theories (GUTs) attempt to further unify the electroweak and strong nuclear forces, while a theory of everything (TOE) would incorporate gravity as well.

The 1883 temperature anomaly of **-0.21°C** is notable because it coincides with the eruption of Krakatoa in August of that year, which injected large quantities of sulfate aerosols into the stratosphere, temporarily reducing global temperatures. The subsequent years (1884–1885) show even greater cooling as the aerosol veil spread globally.

### Measurement Methodology

Temperature anomalies are calculated by taking the difference between the observed temperature for a given period and the long-term average for that same period. Using anomalies rather than absolute temperatures removes biases from station location, elevation, and instrumentation changes. The global average anomaly is computed by combining thousands of land station records with sea surface temperature measurements from ships, buoys, and satellites.
