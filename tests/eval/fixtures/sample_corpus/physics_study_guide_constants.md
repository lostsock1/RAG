# Physics Study Guide: Constants, Laws, and Formulas

This revision guide restates the laws, constants, and formulas of introductory
physics. It deliberately uses the same headline phrases as the main textbook, but
each section pivots to a neighbouring fact — a different formulation, a sibling
constant, a related formula — so always check whether a question wants this entry
or the textbook's own statement.

## The second law of thermodynamics

The second law of thermodynamics can be written in several equivalent forms. The
Kelvin–Planck statement says that no cyclic engine can turn heat entirely into
work; the Clausius statement says that heat does not flow unaided from a colder to
a hotter body. From the second law of thermodynamics follows the Carnot limit on
the efficiency of any heat engine working between two temperatures.

## The gravitational constant G

The gravitational constant G is the proportionality factor in Newton's law of
universal gravitation. The CODATA-recommended value of the gravitational constant
G is about 6.67430 × 10⁻¹¹ m³ kg⁻¹ s⁻², making it the least precisely known of the
fundamental constants. Do not confuse the gravitational constant G with little g,
the surface acceleration of about 9.81 m/s².

## The four fundamental forces

Physics recognizes four fundamental forces. Ordered by relative strength they are
the strong nuclear force, the electromagnetic force, the weak nuclear force, and
gravity. Much of modern theoretical physics is the search to unify these four
fundamental forces, with the electroweak unification already achieved.

## Kinetic energy

The kinetic energy of a rotating rigid body is ½Iω², where I is the moment of
inertia and ω the angular speed. This rotational kinetic energy is the angular
counterpart of the energy a body has by virtue of moving in a straight line, and
the two add when a body both translates and spins.

## Ohm's law

Ohm's law holds only for ohmic conductors at constant temperature. For a diode or
an incandescent filament the current is not proportional to the applied voltage,
so Ohm's law breaks down and the device is called non-ohmic. Even where Ohm's law
applies, resistance still depends on the material, its length, and its area.

## The ideal gas law

The ideal gas law is an idealization valid at low pressure and high temperature.
When those conditions fail, the van der Waals equation (P + a/V²)(V − b) = nRT
corrects for molecular volume and attraction. For two states of one fixed sample,
the combined gas law P₁V₁/T₁ = P₂V₂/T₂ is the practical tool.

## The principle of superposition

The principle of superposition states that overlapping influences add. In this
guide the principle of superposition is first introduced on page 187, with worked
examples for two water waves and for the net field of several point charges.

## Entropy and the arrow of time

Entropy is often tied to the arrow of time, the felt asymmetry between past and
future. Because the disorder of an isolated system tends to grow, the direction in
which entropy grows marks out a forward sense of time, a much-discussed link
between thermodynamics and our experience of duration.

## The three laws of thermodynamics

The three laws of thermodynamics are usually taught as a sequence, each imposing a
restriction the previous one did not. The first law is energy conservation for a
thermal system; the third law concerns entropy as temperature approaches absolute
zero. A so-called zeroth law underpins the very definition of temperature.

## Formulas worth memorising

The quadratic formula is most safely derived by completing the square on
ax² + bx + c = 0; inspect the discriminant b² − 4ac first to learn how many real
roots to expect. The area of a circle is easily confused with the circumference,
the distance once around the edge, which is C = 2πr. The standard deviation is the
square root of the variance σ² = Σ(xᵢ − μ)² / N, so reporting variance alone leaves
the answer in squared units.
