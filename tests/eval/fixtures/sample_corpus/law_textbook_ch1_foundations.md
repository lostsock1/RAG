# Chapter 1: Foundations of Law and the State

## 1.1 Sovereignty in International Law

In the context of international law, sovereignty is the supreme authority of a state to govern itself within its own territory, free from external interference by other states. The concept emerged in its modern form from the Peace of Westphalia in 1648 and remains the organizing principle of the international legal order. Sovereignty has two faces: internal sovereignty, the state's exclusive competence to make and enforce law within its borders, and external sovereignty, the state's formal equality with other states and its capacity to enter into international agreements.

Sovereignty is not absolute in contemporary practice. States voluntarily limit their sovereign powers by treaty — for example, by joining the United Nations, the World Trade Organization, or regional bodies. Customary international law and peremptory norms (jus cogens) bind states regardless of consent.

## 1.2 Forms of Government

### Constitutional Monarchy

A constitutional monarchy is a form of government in which a monarch serves as head of state within the boundaries set by a written or unwritten constitution, while actual governing power is exercised by elected institutions. The monarch's role is largely ceremonial and symbolic: opening parliament, granting formal assent to legislation, and representing continuity of the state. The United Kingdom, Sweden, Japan, and Spain are commonly cited examples. This contrasts with an absolute monarchy, where the monarch holds unconstrained executive authority.

## 1.3 Civil Liability

Civil liability is the legal responsibility of a person or entity to compensate another for harm caused by a wrongful act or omission, enforced through private law remedies rather than criminal punishment. Civil liability arises principally from two sources: breach of contract, where a party fails to perform obligations voluntarily undertaken, and tort, where a duty imposed by law — such as the duty of reasonable care — is violated. The standard remedy is monetary damages intended to place the injured party in the position they would have occupied had the wrong not occurred.

## 1.4 Precedent and the Common Law

In common law systems, precedent is the principle that courts are bound to follow the legal rulings of earlier decisions made by higher courts in the same jurisdiction when the material facts are alike. This doctrine, known formally as stare decisis ("to stand by things decided"), produces predictability and equal treatment: like cases are decided alike. A precedent has two components: the ratio decidendi, the binding legal reasoning necessary to the decision, and obiter dicta, persuasive but non-binding observations. Courts may distinguish a precedent when material facts differ, and apex courts may, exceptionally, overrule their own prior decisions.
