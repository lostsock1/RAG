# Chapter 2: Mathematical Definitions, Formulas, and Structures

## 2.1 Algorithms

An algorithm is a finite, ordered sequence of unambiguous instructions that transforms a given input into a desired output in a finite number of steps. Each step must be precisely defined and executable without creative judgment, and the procedure must terminate. The word derives from the name of the ninth-century Persian mathematician al-Khwarizmi. Long division, Euclid's method for the greatest common divisor, and a cake recipe are all algorithms in this sense; "think of a good move" is not.

## 2.2 Continuity of a Function

A function f is continuous at a point a when three conditions hold simultaneously: first, f(a) is defined; second, the limit of f(x) as x approaches a exists; and third, that limit equals f(a). If any of the three conditions fails, the function is discontinuous at a. Removable discontinuities violate only the third condition, jump discontinuities violate the second, and a function undefined at a violates the first.

## 2.3 Vector Spaces

A vector space is a set of objects called vectors together with two operations, vector addition and scalar multiplication, satisfying eight axioms that guarantee the operations behave consistently. The axioms require associativity and commutativity of addition, an additive identity (the zero vector) and additive inverses, and compatibility, identity, and distributivity laws for scalar multiplication. Familiar examples include the plane R², the space of polynomials of degree at most n, and the space of continuous functions on an interval.

## 2.4 The Quadratic Formula

For a quadratic equation ax² + bx + c = 0 with a ≠ 0, the solutions are given by the quadratic formula: x = (−b ± √(b² − 4ac)) / 2a. The expression b² − 4ac is the discriminant: when positive there are two distinct real roots, when zero one repeated real root, and when negative two complex conjugate roots.

## 2.5 Area of a Circle

The area of a circle is given by A = πr², where r is the radius. The formula follows from integrating the circumference 2πr from 0 to r, or classically from Archimedes' method of exhaustion, which sandwiches the circle between inscribed and circumscribed polygons.

## 2.6 Standard Deviation

The standard deviation of a population is defined as σ = √( Σ(xᵢ − μ)² / N ), the square root of the mean squared deviation from the population mean μ. For a sample, the sum of squared deviations is divided by n − 1 instead of N (Bessel's correction), giving the sample standard deviation s. Standard deviation expresses dispersion in the same units as the data, which is why it is preferred to variance for interpretation.
