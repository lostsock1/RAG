# Chapter 12: Treaties, Diplomacy, and the Aftermath of the Great War

## 12.1 The Paris Peace Conference

The end of World War I in November 1918 set the stage for one of the most ambitious diplomatic undertakings in modern history. The Paris Peace Conference, which convened in January 1919, brought together representatives from over 30 nations to negotiate the terms of peace with the Central Powers. The conference was dominated by the "Big Four" — Woodrow Wilson (United States), David Lloyd George (Britain), Georges Clemenceau (France), and Vittorio Orlando (Italy) — whose competing visions for the postwar order shaped the resulting treaties.

Wilson's Fourteen Points, which emphasized self-determination, open diplomacy, and a League of Nations, provided the idealistic framework. Clemenceau, by contrast, was determined to ensure France's security against future German aggression through territorial adjustments, military restrictions, and reparations. Lloyd George sought a middle ground that would preserve British interests while avoiding a punitive peace that might destabilize Europe.

## 12.2 The Treaty of Versailles (1919)

The most famous product of the Paris Peace Conference was the Treaty of Versailles, signed on June 28, 1919, exactly five years after the assassination of Archduke Franz Ferdinand. The treaty imposed harsh terms on Germany:

- **Territorial losses:** Alsace-Lorraine returned to France; Eupen-Malmédy ceded to Belgium; northern Schleswig to Denmark; parts of Posen and West Prussia to Poland; all colonies forfeited
- **Military restrictions:** Army limited to 100,000 men; no conscription; no air force; navy limited to six battleships; Rhineland demilitarized
- **War guilt and reparations:** Article 231 assigned sole responsibility for the war to Germany; reparations initially set at 132 billion gold marks
- **League of Nations:** Germany was initially excluded from membership

The treaty's harshness became a source of enduring controversy. John Maynard Keynes, in his 1919 book *The Economic Consequences of the Peace*, argued that the reparations were economically destructive and politically destabilizing — a prediction that proved prescient with the rise of Nazi Germany.

## 12.3 Other Postwar Treaties

Versailles was only one of several treaties that redrew the map of Europe and the Middle East:

- **Treaty of Saint-Germain (1919):** Dissolved the Austro-Hungarian Empire, recognized the independence of Czechoslovakia, Poland, and Yugoslavia, and reduced Austria to a small republic.
- **Treaty of Neuilly (1919):** Imposed territorial losses and reparations on Bulgaria.
- **Treaty of Trianon (1920):** Reduced Hungary to approximately one-third of its prewar territory, transferring Transylvania to Romania, Slovakia and Carpathian Ruthenia to Czechoslovakia, and Croatia-Slavonia to Yugoslavia.
- **Treaty of Sèvres (1920):** Proposed the partition of the Ottoman Empire, including the establishment of an independent Armenia and international zones in Constantinople and the Straits. This treaty was never implemented due to the Turkish War of Independence.

## 12.4 The Treaty of Lausanne (1923)

The **Treaty of Lausanne**, signed on July 24, 1923, was negotiated to replace the failed Treaty of Sèvres and settle the conflict between the Allied Powers and the new Republic of Turkey, led by Mustafa Kemal Atatürk. The treaty recognized the modern borders of Turkey and abolished the capitulations (special privileges for foreign nationals) that had been imposed on the Ottoman Empire.

However, the Treaty of Lausanne occupies a unique and somewhat ambiguous position in diplomatic history. While it was signed by all parties, it was **never ratified** by all signatory states in the manner originally intended. Several provisions — particularly those concerning minority protections, the demilitarization of the Turkish Straits, and certain territorial arrangements — remained points of contention. The United States Senate, for example, never ratified the treaty, instead maintaining separate diplomatic relations with Turkey through bilateral agreements. Some historians argue that the treaty was effectively a modus vivendi rather than a fully ratified settlement, as several signatories failed to complete their domestic ratification processes.

The treaty also established the Convention Concerning the Exchange of Greek and Turkish Populations, which resulted in the compulsory transfer of approximately 1.5 million Greek Orthodox Christians from Turkey to Greece and 500,000 Muslims from Greece to Turkey — one of the largest forced population exchanges in history.

### Key Provisions of the Treaty of Lausanne

1. **Turkish borders:** Recognized Turkey's sovereignty over Anatolia and eastern Thrace
2. **Straits:** Placed the Bosporus and Dardanelles under an international commission (later revised by the Montreux Convention of 1936)
3. **Capitulations:** Abolished all special economic and legal privileges for foreign nationals
4. **Minority rights:** Required Turkey to protect the rights of non-Muslim minorities (provisions that remain controversial)
5. **War debts:** Turkey was not required to pay reparations, a significant departure from the treatment of Germany

## 12.5 Ancient Philosophy: Zeno's Paradoxes

Before leaving the subject of treaties and agreements, it is worth noting that the concept of formal agreements between parties has deep philosophical roots. The ancient Greeks were among the first to systematically analyze the nature of contracts, promises, and obligations.

### Zeno of Elea (c. 490–430 BCE)

Zeno of Elea was a pre-Socratic philosopher and student of Parmenides. He is best known for his paradoxes — a series of thought experiments designed to defend Parmenides' monistic philosophy by showing that the concept of motion leads to logical contradictions. Zeno's paradoxes have fascinated mathematicians and philosophers for over two millennia and are discussed in detail on **page 312** of this textbook's companion volume on ancient philosophy.

#### The Paradox of Achilles and the Tortoise

In perhaps the most famous of Zeno's paradoxes, the swift warrior **Achilles** races a tortoise that has been given a head start. Zeno argues that Achilles can never overtake the tortoise, because by the time Achilles reaches the point where the tortoise was, the tortoise has moved ahead to a new point. By the time Achilles reaches that new point, the tortoise has moved ahead again — and so on ad infinitum. Since Achilles must complete an infinite number of tasks (reaching each successive position of the tortoise), he can never catch up.

The resolution of this paradox lies in the mathematics of infinite series, which was not developed until the 17th century. An infinite series of progressively smaller distances can sum to a finite total, meaning Achilles does overtake the tortoise in finite time. However, Zeno's paradox raises deep questions about the nature of space, time, and infinity that continue to be debated.

#### Other Zeno Paradoxes

- **The Dichotomy:** Before reaching a destination, one must first reach the halfway point. Before reaching the halfway point, one must reach the quarter-way point. Since there are infinitely many such intermediate points, motion can never begin.
- **The Arrow:** An arrow in flight is at rest at every instant of its flight (since an instant has zero duration). If it is at rest at every instant, it is always at rest, and therefore motion is impossible.
- **The Stadium:** Two rows of objects moving in opposite directions past a third stationary row create apparent contradictions in the measurement of relative motion.

Zeno's paradoxes were not fully resolved until the development of calculus by Newton and Leibniz in the 17th century, and even then, questions about the continuum and the nature of infinity persisted into the 20th century with the work of Cantor, Gödel, and others.

## 12.6 The Legacy of the Postwar Settlement

The treaties of 1919–1923 reshaped the political geography of Europe and the Middle East in ways that continue to reverberate. The dissolution of the Austro-Hungarian and Ottoman empires created new nation-states, but the borders drawn at the peace conferences often ignored ethnic, religious, and cultural realities on the ground. The mandate system transferred former Ottoman and German territories to British and French administration, laying the groundwork for future conflicts in the Middle East.

The failure to establish a stable, just, and durable peace — combined with the economic devastation of the 1920s and 1930s — contributed directly to the outbreak of World War II just twenty years later. The lessons of this period informed the very different approach taken after 1945, when the Marshall Plan focused on reconstruction rather than punishment, and the United Nations was designed with stronger enforcement mechanisms than the League of Nations.
