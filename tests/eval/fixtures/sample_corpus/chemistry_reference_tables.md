# Chemistry Reference: Constants, Tables, and Properties

## R.1 Physical Constants of Common Substances

The boiling point of water at standard atmospheric pressure (1 atm) is 100 °C, which is equal to 212 °F. This reference point, together with the freezing point at 0 °C (32 °F), historically defined the Celsius scale.

Selected melting points of refractory metals:

| Metal | Symbol | Melting point (°C) |
|---|---|---|
| Iron | Fe | 1538 |
| Titanium | Ti | 1668 |
| Molybdenum | Mo | 2623 |
| Tungsten | W | 3422 |

Tungsten has the highest melting point of all metals, at 3422 °C, which is why it is used for incandescent filaments and rocket nozzle throats.

## R.2 Electronegativity (Pauling Scale)

| Element | Electronegativity |
|---|---|
| Fluorine | 3.98 |
| Oxygen | 3.44 |
| Chlorine | 3.16 |
| Nitrogen | 3.04 |

Fluorine has the highest electronegativity of all elements, with a Pauling value of 3.98. Electronegativity decreases down a group and increases across a period.

## R.3 Selected Atomic Numbers

| Element | Symbol | Atomic number |
|---|---|---|
| Oxygen | O | 8 |
| Silver | Ag | 47 |
| Gold | Au | 79 |
| Lead | Pb | 82 |

The atomic number of gold is 79, meaning each gold atom carries 79 protons in its nucleus.

## R.4 SI Base Units

| Quantity | Unit | Symbol |
|---|---|---|
| Length | metre | m |
| Mass | kilogram | kg |
| Time | second | s |
| Electric current | ampere | A |
| Temperature | kelvin | K |

The SI base unit of electric current is the ampere, with the symbol A. Since the 2019 redefinition, the ampere is fixed by assigning an exact value to the elementary charge.

## R.5 Solubility Rules

Most chlorides are soluble in water; the important exceptions are silver, lead(II), and mercury(I) chlorides. Silver chloride (AgCl) is insoluble in water, with a solubility product constant (Ksp) of only 1.77 × 10⁻¹⁰ at 25 °C. The formation of a white AgCl precipitate when silver nitrate is added is the classic qualitative test for chloride ions in solution.
