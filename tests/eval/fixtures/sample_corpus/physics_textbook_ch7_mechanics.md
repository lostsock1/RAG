# Chapter 7: Classical Mechanics and Waves

## 7.1 Kinetic Energy

The kinetic energy of a moving object is the energy it possesses by virtue of its motion. For an object of mass m moving at speed v, the physics textbook gives the formula KE = ½mv². Kinetic energy is a scalar quantity measured in joules, always non-negative, and proportional to the square of the speed — doubling the speed quadruples the kinetic energy, which is why stopping distances grow so steeply with velocity.

## 7.2 The Principle of Superposition

The principle of superposition is first introduced on page 204 of this textbook, in the section on wave interference. It states that when two or more waves overlap in the same medium, the resultant displacement at any point is the algebraic sum of the displacements that each wave would produce individually. Superposition explains constructive interference, destructive interference, standing waves, and beats, and it applies wherever the governing equations are linear.

## 7.3 Three Laws of Thermodynamics

This section presents the three laws of thermodynamics as a connected hierarchy. The first law states that energy is conserved: the change in a system's internal energy equals the heat added minus the work done by the system. The second law states that the total entropy of an isolated system never decreases, establishing a direction for spontaneous processes. The third law states that as a system approaches absolute zero, its entropy approaches a constant minimum. Each law builds on the previous: the first defines energy bookkeeping, the second constrains which energy transformations are possible, and the third fixes the absolute reference point for entropy.

## 7.4 Wave Speed

The speed of a wave on a stretched string depends on the tension and the linear mass density: v = √(T/μ), where T is the tension in newtons and μ is the mass per unit length. A tighter or lighter string carries waves faster — the physical basis for tuning a stringed instrument.
