# Chapter 2: Markets, Prices, and Macroeconomic Measurement

## 2.1 Market Equilibrium

A market is any arrangement that brings buyers and sellers together to exchange goods, services, or resources. The interaction of supply and demand determines the market price and quantity of goods traded. Market equilibrium occurs at the price where the quantity demanded equals the quantity supplied — there is no surplus or shortage.

When the market price is above equilibrium, a surplus exists: sellers wish to sell more than buyers wish to purchase, putting downward pressure on prices. When the price is below equilibrium, a shortage exists: buyers wish to purchase more than sellers wish to sell, putting upward pressure on prices. In competitive markets, these pressures drive the market toward equilibrium.

### Shifts in Supply and Demand

Changes in underlying conditions can shift the supply or demand curves:

- **Demand shifters:** Consumer income, preferences, prices of related goods (substitutes and complements), population, and expectations about future prices.
- **Supply shifters:** Input costs, technology, number of sellers, expectations, and government policies (taxes, subsidies, regulations).

When demand increases (shifts right), both equilibrium price and quantity rise. When supply increases (shifts right), equilibrium quantity rises but price falls. The simultaneous shifting of both curves produces outcomes that depend on the relative magnitudes of the shifts.

## 2.2 Measuring Economic Performance

Macroeconomics examines the economy as a whole. Key indicators of economic performance include gross domestic product (GDP), unemployment rate, and the rate of inflation. These indicators help policymakers, businesses, and households make informed decisions.

### Gross Domestic Product

GDP is the total market value of all final goods and services produced within a country's borders in a given time period. It can be measured using the expenditure approach:

> GDP = C + I + G + (X − M)

where C is consumption, I is investment, G is government spending, and (X − M) is net exports.

GDP can be measured in nominal terms (current prices) or real terms (adjusted for price changes). Real GDP provides a more accurate picture of economic growth because it removes the effect of price level changes.

## 2.3 Inflation

**Inflation** is defined as a **general increase** in the level of **prices** across an economy over a period of time. When the overall price level rises, each unit of currency buys fewer goods and services, which means that **purchasing power** declines. Inflation is typically expressed as an annual percentage rate — for example, an inflation rate of 3% means that, on average, prices are 3% higher than they were one year ago.

### Measuring Inflation

Inflation is most commonly measured using a price index, which tracks the cost of a representative basket of goods and services over time. The two most widely used price indices are:

1. **Consumer Price Index (CPI):** Measures the average change in prices paid by urban consumers for a market basket of consumer goods and services. The CPI is the most commonly cited measure of inflation and is used to adjust wages, pensions, and tax brackets.

2. **GDP Deflator:** A broader measure that captures price changes for all goods and services included in GDP, including investment goods and government services. Unlike the CPI, the GDP deflator's basket changes automatically as the composition of GDP changes.

The inflation rate is calculated as:

> Inflation rate = (Price index in year t − Price index in year t−1) / Price index in year t−1 × 100%

### Types of Inflation

Economists distinguish several types of inflation based on its rate and causes:

- **Demand-pull inflation:** Occurs when aggregate demand exceeds aggregate supply at full employment. "Too much money chasing too few goods." Often associated with expansionary monetary or fiscal policy.

- **Cost-push inflation:** Occurs when production costs rise (e.g., higher oil prices, increased wages), causing firms to raise prices even without excess demand. Supply shocks are a common cause.

- **Built-in (wage-price) inflation:** A self-perpetuating cycle in which workers demand higher wages to keep up with rising prices, and firms raise prices to cover higher wage costs. This creates an inflationary spiral.

- **Hyperinflation:** Extremely rapid inflation, typically exceeding 50% per month. Historical examples include Germany (1921–1923), Zimbabwe (2007–2009), and Venezuela (2016–2019). Hyperinflation destroys the function of money as a store of value and medium of exchange.

- **Deflation:** A general decrease in the price level. While falling prices may seem beneficial, deflation can be economically destructive because it increases the real burden of debt, discourages spending (consumers delay purchases expecting lower prices), and can lead to a deflationary spiral.

### Consequences of Inflation

Inflation affects different groups in different ways:

1. **Purchasing power:** The most direct effect — a dollar buys less. Fixed-income earners (retirees on pensions) are particularly vulnerable.

2. **Redistribution of wealth:** Inflation redistributes wealth from lenders to borrowers because loans are repaid in dollars that are worth less than when the loans were made.

3. **Uncertainty:** High or unpredictable inflation makes long-term planning difficult for businesses and households, discouraging investment.

4. **Menu costs:** Firms incur costs from frequently changing prices (reprinting menus, catalogs, price tags).

5. **Tax distortions:** Inflation can push taxpayers into higher tax brackets even if their real income has not increased (bracket creep), though many tax systems now index brackets to inflation.

### Central Bank Response

Central banks, such as the Federal Reserve in the United States and the European Central Bank, use monetary policy to manage inflation. Their primary tool is the interest rate:

- To combat high inflation, central banks raise interest rates, making borrowing more expensive and slowing economic activity.
- To fight deflation or stimulate the economy, central banks lower interest rates to encourage borrowing and spending.

Most major central banks target an inflation rate of approximately 2%, which is considered consistent with stable prices and healthy economic growth.

## 2.4 Unemployment

The unemployment rate measures the percentage of the labor force that is actively seeking work but unable to find employment. Economists classify unemployment into three types:

1. **Frictional unemployment:** Short-term unemployment that occurs when workers are between jobs or entering the labor force for the first time. This type is always present in a dynamic economy.

2. **Structural unemployment:** Occurs when workers' skills do not match the skills demanded by employers, often due to technological change, globalization, or industry decline.

3. **Cyclical unemployment:** Results from downturns in the business cycle. During recessions, demand for goods and services falls, leading firms to lay off workers.

The natural rate of unemployment (the sum of frictional and structural unemployment) is typically estimated at 4–5% in developed economies. Full employment does not mean zero unemployment — it means cyclical unemployment is zero.

## 2.5 The Phillips Curve

The Phillips curve describes an inverse relationship between inflation and unemployment in the short run. When unemployment is low, inflation tends to be higher (tight labor markets push up wages and prices). When unemployment is high, inflation tends to be lower.

In the long run, most economists believe the Phillips curve is vertical at the natural rate of unemployment — there is no permanent trade-off between inflation and unemployment. Attempts to keep unemployment below its natural rate through expansionary policy lead only to accelerating inflation.

Understanding these concepts is essential for analyzing economic conditions and evaluating policy decisions.
