# Chapter 3: Microeconomic Concepts and Macroeconomic Relationships

## 3.1 Elasticity

Elasticity measures the responsiveness of one economic variable to a change in another, expressed as the ratio of percentage changes. The most commonly used measure is the price elasticity of demand: the percentage change in quantity demanded divided by the percentage change in price. When the absolute value exceeds 1, demand is elastic — consumers respond strongly to price changes; below 1, demand is inelastic. Goods with close substitutes (restaurant meals) tend to be elastic, while necessities with few substitutes (insulin, gasoline in the short run) tend to be inelastic.

## 3.2 Opportunity Cost

Opportunity cost is the value of the next-best alternative that must be given up when a choice is made. It is the foundational concept of economic reasoning: because resources are scarce, every decision carries a cost measured not in money spent but in alternatives forgone. A student who spends an evening studying gives up the wages from a shift at work; a government that builds a stadium gives up the hospital that the same funds could have financed. Rational decision-making compares the expected benefit of an option against its opportunity cost, not merely its accounting cost.

## 3.3 Market Equilibrium

Market equilibrium is the state in which the quantity supplied equals the quantity demanded at the prevailing market price, leaving no tendency for the price to change. Graphically, it is the intersection of the supply and demand curves. At any price above equilibrium, a surplus emerges and sellers bid prices down; at any price below, a shortage emerges and buyers bid prices up. Equilibrium is therefore self-restoring in competitive markets — a property Adam Smith described as the invisible hand.

## 3.4 Okun's Law

Okun's law describes the empirical relationship between unemployment and output: for every percentage point by which the unemployment rate exceeds its natural rate, real GDP falls roughly two percentage points below its potential. First documented by Arthur Okun in 1962 using United States data, the relationship is a rule of thumb rather than a structural law — the coefficient varies across countries and decades. Okun's law links the labor market to the goods market and explains why recessions, which raise unemployment, produce disproportionately large losses in national output.
