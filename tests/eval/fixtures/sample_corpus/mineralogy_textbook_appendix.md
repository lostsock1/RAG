# Appendix C: Crystal Symmetry and Mineral Classification

## C.1 Introduction to Crystallography

Crystallography is the science of crystal structure and symmetry. A crystal is a solid material whose constituent atoms, molecules, or ions are arranged in an orderly, repeating pattern extending in all three spatial dimensions. The study of crystal symmetry is fundamental to mineralogy because the external shape of a crystal reflects its internal atomic arrangement.

All crystals can be classified into one of seven crystal systems based on their symmetry elements:

1. **Triclinic:** No symmetry axes or planes (lowest symmetry)
2. **Monoclinic:** One twofold axis and/or one mirror plane
3. **Orthorhombic:** Three mutually perpendicular twofold axes and/or mirror planes
4. **Tetragonal:** One fourfold axis
5. **Trigonal:** One threefold axis
6. **Hexagonal:** One sixfold axis
7. **Cubic (Isometric):** Four threefold axes arranged as the body diagonals of a cube (highest symmetry)

## C.2 Symmetry Operations and Point Groups

A symmetry operation is a movement of a crystal that maps it onto itself — that is, the crystal looks identical before and after the operation. The basic symmetry operations are:

- **Rotation:** Rotation about an axis by a specific angle (e.g., 2-fold, 3-fold, 4-fold, 6-fold)
- **Reflection:** Reflection through a mirror plane
- **Inversion:** Reflection through a center of symmetry
- **Rotoinversion:** A combination of rotation and inversion

The complete set of symmetry operations that can be performed on a crystal defines its **point group** (also called crystal class). There are 32 possible point groups in three-dimensional crystallography.

### Fold Symmetry

The "fold" of a symmetry axis refers to the number of times a crystal must be rotated (through 360°/n) to return to its original appearance:

- **2-fold:** 180° rotation
- **3-fold:** 120° rotation
- **4-fold:** 90° rotation
- **6-fold:** 60° rotation
- **8-fold:** 45° rotation (note: true 8-fold rotational symmetry is not possible in periodic crystal lattices, but can appear in certain crystal forms and quasicrystals)

## C.3 The Garnet Group

The garnet group comprises a family of nesosilicate minerals with the general formula X₃Y₂(SiO₄)₃, where X and Y represent different cation sites. Garnets are common in metamorphic rocks and are widely used as gemstones and industrial abrasives.

### Common Garnet Species

| Almandine | Fe₃Al₂(SiO₄)₃ | Deep red | Cubic |
| Pyrope | Mg₃Al₂(SiO₄)₃ | Red to purplish-red | Cubic |

| Grossular | Ca₃Al₂(SiO₄)₃ | Colorless, green, yellow, brown | Cubic |

| Uvarovite | Ca₃Cr₂(SiO₄)₃ | Emerald green | Cubic |

### Grossular Garnet and Symmetry

**Grossular garnet** (Ca₃Al₂(SiO₄)₃) is a particularly interesting member of the garnet group. It crystallizes in the cubic system and commonly forms well-developed dodecahedral and trapezohedral crystals. The dodecahedral form exhibits a distinctive **8-fold** symmetry when viewed along certain crystallographic directions — specifically, the [111] body diagonal reveals an arrangement of faces that demonstrates octahedral symmetry elements.

This **8-fold** symmetry in grossular garnet is a consequence of the cubic crystal system's high degree of symmetry. The rhombic dodecahedron, one of the most common crystal forms for grossular, has 12 rhomb-shaped faces. When viewed along the [110] direction, the arrangement of faces creates an apparent **8-fold** rotational pattern that is characteristic of this mineral's growth habit.

Grossular garnet occurs in a range of colors depending on trace element substitutions:

- **Hessonite:** Orange to brown variety (iron-bearing)
- **Tsavorite:** Vivid green variety (vanadium/chromium-bearing), highly prized as a gemstone
- **Leuco-grossular:** Colorless to pale variety

The mineral is found primarily in contact metamorphosed limestones (skarns) and in some regionally metamorphosed rocks. Notable occurrences include the Jeffrey Mine in Quebec (Canada), the Vilyui River region in Siberia (Russia), and deposits in Kenya and Tanzania (source of tsavorite).

## C.4 Quasicrystals and Forbidden Symmetry

Traditional crystallography held that only 1-, 2-, 3-, 4-, and 6-fold rotational symmetry was possible in crystals. This restriction arises from the requirement that crystals fill space through periodic tiling. However, in 1982, Dan Shechtman discovered an aluminum-manganese alloy with sharp diffraction spots and apparent **5-fold** symmetry — a "forbidden" symmetry that could not exist in a periodic lattice.

This discovery led to the recognition of **quasicrystals** — ordered but non-periodic structures. Quasicrystals can exhibit symmetries that are impossible in periodic crystals, including 5-fold, 8-fold, 10-fold, and 12-fold rotational symmetry. Shechtman was awarded the Nobel Prize in Chemistry in 2011 for this discovery.

While true 8-fold rotational symmetry cannot occur in periodic crystal lattices, it can appear in:

1. **Octagonal quasicrystals:** Rare quasicrystalline phases with 8-fold symmetry
2. **Crystal forms:** The external shape of certain cubic minerals (like grossular garnet) can display apparent 8-fold symmetry when viewed along specific directions
3. **Twin patterns:** Contact twins or cyclic twins can produce composite forms with higher apparent symmetry than the individual crystals

## C.5 Mineral Identification Using Symmetry

Symmetry is one of the most powerful tools for mineral identification. The process typically involves:

1. **Observing crystal habit:** The characteristic shape in which a mineral grows (e.g., cubic, prismatic, tabular, bladed)
2. **Identifying symmetry elements:** Looking for mirror planes, rotation axes, and centers of symmetry
3. **Measuring angles:** Using a contact goniometer to measure interfacial angles, which are constant for a given mineral species
4. **Confirming with optical properties:** Polarized light microscopy can reveal internal symmetry through optical indicatrix behavior
