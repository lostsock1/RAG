# Chapter 5: Cell Biology — Energy and Metabolism

## 5.1 Energy in Living Systems

All living organisms require energy to maintain their complex organization, grow, reproduce, and respond to their environment. The study of how cells obtain and use energy is called bioenergetics. At the cellular level, energy transformations are governed by the same laws of thermodynamics that apply to physical systems.

The primary source of energy for nearly all life on Earth is the Sun. Organisms that can capture light energy and convert it into chemical energy are called **photoautotrophs**, and the process by which they do this is called **photosynthesis**. Organisms that cannot perform photosynthesis obtain energy by consuming other organisms and breaking down their organic molecules through cellular respiration.

## 5.2 Photosynthesis

**Photosynthesis** is the biochemical process by which green plants, algae, and certain bacteria convert **light energy** from the Sun into **chemical energy** stored in the bonds of organic molecules, primarily glucose. This process is fundamental to life on Earth, as it produces the organic compounds that form the base of virtually all food chains and generates the oxygen that most organisms need for respiration.

### The Overall Equation

The overall chemical equation for photosynthesis can be summarized as:

> **6CO₂ + 6H₂O + light energy → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂**

In words: six molecules of **carbon dioxide** and six molecules of water, using **light energy**, produce one molecule of **glucose** (C₆H₁₂O₆) and six molecules of oxygen.

### The Role of Chlorophyll

**Chlorophyll** is the primary photosynthetic pigment responsible for absorbing light energy. It is located in the thylakoid membranes of chloroplasts in plant cells and algae. Chlorophyll molecules absorb light most efficiently in the blue-violet and red regions of the visible spectrum and reflect green light, which is why plants appear green.

There are several types of chlorophyll:

- **Chlorophyll a:** The primary photosynthetic pigment found in all photosynthetic eukaryotes and cyanobacteria. It participates directly in the light-dependent reactions.
- **Chlorophyll b:** An accessory pigment that absorbs light at slightly different wavelengths and transfers the energy to chlorophyll a.
- **Carotenoids:** Accessory pigments (including carotenes and xanthophylls) that absorb blue and green light, extending the range of wavelengths that can be used for photosynthesis. They also protect chlorophyll from photooxidative damage.

### The Two Stages of Photosynthesis

Photosynthesis occurs in two main stages:

#### Stage 1: Light-Dependent Reactions

The light-dependent reactions (also called the light reactions) take place in the thylakoid membranes of the chloroplast. When chlorophyll absorbs light energy, electrons are excited to higher energy states and passed through an electron transport chain. This process:

1. Splits water molecules (photolysis), releasing oxygen as a byproduct
2. Generates ATP through chemiosmosis (photophosphorylation)
3. Produces NADPH, an electron carrier

There are two photosystems involved:

- **Photosystem II (PSII):** Absorbs light at 680 nm, initiates electron flow, and splits water
- **Photosystem I (PSI):** Absorbs light at 700 nm, generates NADPH

#### Stage 2: The Calvin Cycle (Light-Independent Reactions)

The Calvin cycle (also called the dark reactions or light-independent reactions) takes place in the stroma of the chloroplast. It uses the ATP and NADPH produced by the light reactions to fix carbon dioxide into organic molecules. The cycle consists of three phases:

1. **Carbon fixation:** CO₂ is incorporated into a five-carbon sugar (ribulose-1,5-bisphosphate, RuBP) by the enzyme RuBisCO, producing an unstable six-carbon intermediate that immediately splits into two molecules of 3-phosphoglycerate (3-PGA).

2. **Reduction:** 3-PGA is phosphorylated by ATP and then reduced by NADPH to form glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate (G3P), a three-carbon sugar.

3. **Regeneration:** Most G3P molecules are used to regenerate RuBP so the cycle can continue. For every three CO₂ molecules fixed, one net G3P is produced. Two G3P molecules combine to form one glucose molecule.

### Factors Affecting Photosynthesis

Several environmental factors influence the rate of photosynthesis:

- **Light intensity:** Rate increases with light intensity up to a saturation point
- **Carbon dioxide concentration:** Higher CO₂ levels generally increase the rate
- **Temperature:** Optimal range is typically 25–35°C for most plants; extreme temperatures denature enzymes
- **Water availability:** Water is a reactant and also affects stomatal opening

### Importance of Photosynthesis

Photosynthesis is arguably the most important biochemical process on Earth:

- It produces the oxygen that aerobic organisms need for respiration
- It forms the base of nearly all food chains
- It removes CO₂ from the atmosphere, helping to regulate climate
- Ancient photosynthesis produced the fossil fuels that power modern civilization

## 5.3 Cellular Respiration

Cellular respiration is the complementary process to photosynthesis. It breaks down glucose to release the chemical energy stored in its bonds, producing ATP — the universal energy currency of cells.

The overall equation for aerobic respiration is:

> C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ → 6CO₂ + 6H₂O + ATP

Cellular respiration occurs in three main stages:

1. **Glycolysis:** Occurs in the cytoplasm; splits glucose into two pyruvate molecules, producing a net 2 ATP and 2 NADH.

2. **The Krebs cycle (citric acid cycle):** Occurs in the mitochondrial matrix; completely oxidizes the products of glycolysis, generating NADH, FADH₂, and 2 ATP per glucose molecule.

3. **Oxidative phosphorylation:** Occurs on the inner mitochondrial membrane; uses the electron transport chain and chemiosmosis to produce approximately 34 ATP per glucose molecule.

The theoretical maximum yield of aerobic respiration is approximately 38 ATP per glucose molecule, though the actual yield is typically 30–32 ATP due to losses from membrane transport.

## 5.4 ATP: The Energy Currency

Adenosine triphosphate (ATP) is the primary energy carrier in all living cells. It consists of an adenine base, a ribose sugar, and three phosphate groups. The energy stored in ATP is released when the terminal phosphate bond is hydrolyzed:

> ATP → ADP + Pᵢ + energy

The hydrolysis of one ATP molecule releases approximately 30.5 kJ/mol of energy under standard conditions. Cells continuously regenerate ATP from ADP and inorganic phosphate using energy from photosynthesis (in plants) or cellular respiration (in all organisms).

## Chapter Summary

This chapter examined how cells capture, store, and use energy. Photosynthesis converts light energy into chemical energy stored in glucose, using chlorophyll to absorb light and carbon dioxide as a carbon source. The overall reaction — 6CO₂ + 6H₂O → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ — sustains virtually all life on Earth. Cellular respiration reverses this process, breaking down glucose to produce ATP. Together, these two processes form the energetic foundation of the biosphere.
