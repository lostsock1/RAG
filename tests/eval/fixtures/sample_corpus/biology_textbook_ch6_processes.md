# Chapter 6: Cellular Processes and Homeostasis

## 6.1 Homeostasis

Homeostasis is the maintenance of a stable internal environment by an organism despite changes in external conditions, achieved through self-regulating feedback mechanisms. Body temperature, blood pH, blood glucose, and water balance are all held within narrow ranges by negative feedback loops, in which a deviation from the set point triggers a corrective response that returns the variable toward that set point. The regulation of human body temperature near 37 °C by sweating and shivering is the canonical example.

## 6.2 The Endoplasmic Reticulum and the Golgi Apparatus

The endoplasmic reticulum (ER) and the Golgi apparatus form a coordinated secretory pathway. The rough ER, studded with ribosomes, synthesizes and folds proteins; these proteins are packaged into transport vesicles that bud from the ER and travel to the Golgi apparatus. The Golgi apparatus then modifies, sorts, and packages these proteins — adding carbohydrate groups and tagging them for their destination — before dispatching them in vesicles to the cell surface, the lysosomes, or secretion. The ER manufactures; the Golgi finishes and ships. Disruption of this pathway underlies several human diseases.

## 6.3 Mitosis and Meiosis

Mitosis and meiosis are both forms of nuclear division, but they serve opposite purposes. Mitosis produces two genetically identical diploid daughter cells from one parent cell and underlies growth, repair, and asexual reproduction. Meiosis produces four genetically distinct haploid gametes through two successive divisions, halving the chromosome number and generating variation through crossing over and independent assortment. Mitosis preserves the genome; meiosis shuffles and halves it for sexual reproduction.

## 6.4 Adaptive Radiation: A Case Study

The chapter's case study in adaptive radiation is the marine iguana of the Galápagos Islands, an unusual reptile that, unlike all other iguanas, forages underwater for algae. Isolated on the archipelago, ancestral land iguanas radiated into a marine niche, evolving flattened tails for swimming, blunt snouts for grazing algae off rocks, and specialized glands that expel excess salt. The marine iguana illustrates how an empty ecological niche drives rapid morphological divergence from a common ancestor.

## 6.5 Enzyme Exceptions

Most enzymes obey the lock-and-key or induced-fit model, binding a specific substrate at an active site. The chapter notes one striking exception discussed only here: lysozyme, an enzyme that strains its bound substrate toward the transition state by distorting the sugar ring, rather than simply complementing the substrate's ground-state shape. This distortion mechanism was the first to be revealed by X-ray crystallography of an enzyme–substrate complex.
